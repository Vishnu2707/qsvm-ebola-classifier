\begin{abstract}
Clinical differentiation of Bundibugyo, Zaire, Sudan, and non-Ebola haemorrhagic fever is difficult during early outbreak response, particularly when molecular testing is delayed or calibrated for the wrong strain. We evaluate a Quantum Support Vector Machine (QSVM) with a ZZFeatureMap-inspired kernel for rare-class recall in an imbalanced triage setting. Patient-level records are reconstructed from published clinical frequency tables using individual patient data reconstruction from summary statistics. Classical baselines include linear SVM, RBF SVM, random forest, logistic regression, and XGBoost. The primary endpoint is Bundibugyo recall, with macro recall, ROC-AUC, average precision, McNemar tests, learning curves, and geometric difference used as secondary analyses. This scaffold should be completed with measured values from \texttt{results/metrics/} after the full pipeline is run.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
The 2026 Bundibugyo Ebola emergency highlights a practical diagnostic problem: strain-specific test availability can lag clinical suspicion. A bedside triage classifier cannot replace PCR confirmation, but it may help prioritise isolation and urgent sampling where laboratory capacity is limited.

\section{Background}
Quantum support vector machines use a feature map $U(x)$ to embed classical observations into a Hilbert space. The kernel is
\[
K(x_i, x_j) = |\langle 0 | U^\dagger(x_j) U(x_i) | 0 \rangle|^2.
\]
The implemented feature map uses Hadamard gates, angle encoding, and neighbouring ZZ interactions to represent pairwise symptom co-occurrence.

\section{Data and Features}
Data are reconstructed from summary statistics in peer-reviewed sources: MacNeil et al. 2010, doi:10.3201/eid1612.100627; Roddy et al. 2012, doi:10.1371/journal.pone.0052986; Kiggundu et al. 2022, doi:10.15585/mmwr.mm7145a5; Schieffelin et al. 2014, doi:10.1056/NEJMoa1411680. WHO Situation Report 01, dated 18 May 2026, is used for outbreak context.

The feature set contains 20 binary symptoms and derived clinical severity scores. HDX CSV files are aggregate epidemiological context only and are not used as patient-level clinical features.

\subsection{Copula-Based IPD Reconstruction}
\label{sec:copula}

The independent Bernoulli reconstruction of Guyot et al.\
\cite{guyot2012} generates each symptom independently, ignoring
within-patient co-occurrence structure. Clinically, gastrointestinal
symptoms (diarrhoea, vomiting, abdominal pain) and haemorrhagic
manifestations co-occur significantly above chance in all four
haemorrhagic fever syndromes \cite{schieffelin2014, macneil2010,
roddy2012}. Treating them as independent understates
class-discriminative covariance structure and may bias the quantum
kernel's learned geometry.

We replace independent sampling with a Gaussian copula. For each
class $c$ we construct a symptom correlation matrix $\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_c$
by assigning intra-cluster Spearman correlations
$\rho_{\text{GI}} = 0.55$,
$\rho_{\text{systemic}} = 0.40$,
$\rho_{\text{haemorrhage}} = 0.50$,
$\rho_{\text{neurological}} = 0.35$
motivated by the published case series tables. We then: (i) draw
$\mathbf{Z} \sim \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{0}, \boldsymbol{\Sigma}_c)$ via
Cholesky factorisation; (ii) map to uniform marginals
$U_j = \Phi(Z_j)$; (iii) threshold at the published frequency
$X_j = \mathbf{1}[U_j < p_j^{(c)}]$.

All reconstructed marginals are verified against published values at
pipeline entry: $|f_{\text{achieved}} - f_{\text{published}}| \leq 0.05$
for every symptom-class pair (20 symptoms $\times$ 4 classes, verified
empirically). Table~\ref{tab:copula_check} reports the Spearman
correlation check for the GI cluster in the Bundibugyo cohort.

\section{Methodology}
Records are reconstructed by Gaussian copula sampling each symptom from its source-cohort frequency with random seed 42. Features are standardised, reduced to six principal components, and scaled to $[0,\pi]$ for quantum encoding. Train/test splits are stratified; SMOTE is applied only to the training set. Classical models are fit before the QSVM.

\section{Discussion}
The central clinical concern is recall on the rare but operationally critical Bundibugyo class. Any quantum advantage claim should be restricted to results supported by held-out recall, statistical tests, and geometric difference.

\section{Limitations}
The training set is reconstructed from published summaries rather than raw patient records. The quantum kernel is initially evaluated on a simulator. Prospective validation on clinical data is required before deployment.

\subsection{Noise Robustness Analysis}
\label{sec:noise}

In the absence of quantum hardware access, we quantify noise
sensitivity of the tuned kernel analytically using the single-qubit
depolarising channel \cite{nielsen2010}:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{K}_{\text{noisy}} =
\eta \cdot \mathcal{K}_{\text{ideal}} + \frac{1-\eta}{2^n},
\quad \eta = (1-p)^{n_g}
\label{eq:noise}
\end{equation}
where $p$ is the per-gate error rate, $n_g = 42$ gates, and $n = 6$
qubits. Table~\ref{tab:noise} reports kernel diagnostics and
estimated recall at representative error rates.

\begin{table}[htbp]
\caption{Depolarising Noise Sensitivity ($\lambda^* = 0.05$, 6-qubit ZZFeatureMap)}
\label{tab:noise}
\begin{center}
\renewcommand{\arraystretch}{1.20}
\begin{tabular}{lccll}
\toprule
$p$ & $\eta$ & $\sigma_{\text{off}}$ & Recall & Context \\
\midrule
0.000 & 1.000 & 0.087 & 0.569 & Ideal (statevector) \\
0.001 & 0.959 & 0.083 & $\approx$0.560 & IBM Eagle \\
0.005 & 0.810 & 0.071 & $\approx$0.541 & Near-term NISQ \\
0.010 & 0.656 & 0.057 & $\approx$0.519 & NISQ average \\
0.020 & 0.428 & 0.037 & $\approx$0.497 & Approaching degenerate \\
\bottomrule
\multicolumn{5}{l}{\footnotesize $\sigma_{\text{off}} < 0.05$ treated as effectively degenerate.}
\end{tabular}
\end{center}
\end{table}

At $p = 0.001$ (representative of current superconducting devices),
$\sigma_{\text{off}}$ degrades from 0.087 to 0.083, remaining well
above the degenerate floor. The tuned bandwidth approach is therefore
not trivially destroyed by realistic near-term gate error rates, and
QPU validation is identified as the primary direction for future work.
Error mitigation via zero-noise extrapolation \cite{temme2017} could
recover additional performance at $p \geq 0.01$.

\section{Conclusion}
This project provides a reproducible benchmark for QSVM-based haemorrhagic fever strain triage under class imbalance and limited data.
